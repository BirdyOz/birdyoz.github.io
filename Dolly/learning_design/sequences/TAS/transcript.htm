Certificate II in Business

TAFE Tasmania with Tasmanian Communities Online – John Tucker, Jon Paice

1. Background

Tasmanian Communities Online is a network of 66 Online Access Centres recurrently funded and centrally supported through Department of Education (Centre Support Unit)
Our Project in 2005 was called Embedding Effective Local Learning Support for E-Learners in Rural Communities. The project had a primary goal to create and trial a model of support for students, especially those of mature age, who are re-entering education and training, in their local community.
This involved building on an existing e-delivery partnership between Online Access Centres, TAFE Tasmania, and the Department of Education to deliver a Certificate II in Business course locally with a combination of e-learning, work experience and face to face coaching.

2. Challenges

A major challenge for us was to find a way to support learning in remote and disadvantaged communities, especially for mature aged unemployed people and single parents wanting to enter or re-enter the workforce.
“For many potential learners, beginning study at a mature age or after having children is daunting, especially for those in rural communities disadvantaged by isolation and limited post secondary learning opportunities,” Mr Norris.

3. Solution: the model

Our project model worked like this: in partnership with TAFE Tasmania, Tasmanian Communities Online delivered Certificate II in Business to students around Tasmania. TAFE provided course materials and online support. The Centre Support Unit, which is a section of the Education Department that manages the TCO Network, set up the model and provided support, coordination and oversight of the arrangements. The local Online Access Centres were the interface between TAFE and the students.
The model that we have developed supports e-learning and enhances the capacity of the learner to be involved in Life-Long Learning.  
At the most basic level our project delivered a Certificate II in Business course chosen because, as well as utilising eLearning in its delivery, it also introduces many prerequisite e-learning skills. But we also had another set of learners, in the coordinators of the Online Access Centres. Professional development supported through wikis and discussion forums utilising EdNA Groups was important. These coordinators were the facilitators of the STUDY CIRCLES which are the keystones for support and personal growth of our e-learners.

4. The course

The course, Certificate II in Business Studies, was offered to volunteers in Tasmanian Online Access Centres.
There are 12 units for the qualification. Four units were supported by hardcopy and CD-ROM based resources.

Learning activities for the other units were specifically designed to reflect the typical activities of staff in an Online Access Centre. A minimum of 80 hours of volunteer work activity in a centre over a period of no less than 12 weeks up to 24 weeks was required to provide assessors sufficient opportunity to collect evidence of workplace competence.

The course was supported by online forums, discussions and email, as well as by face-to-face Study Circle groups.

Supporting learners in a face-to-face Study Circle was an integral part of the course requirements for the students - having the dual purpose of

  1. providing a supportive environment and
  2. being a vehicle to present elements of the course for assessment.

Many of the disadvantages that are attributed to distance or online learning can be overcome with local support provided by the Study Circle model and our Online Access Centres (or any local organisation with access to PCs).
So what are Study Circles?
They are meetings where people sit, usually in a circle and typically with a cup of tea and cake – because this is all about making people feel comfortable and putting people in touch with others like themselves.
What Happens in a Study Circle?
The topics that are discussed in a study circle will vary widely from group to group but generally discussions will gravitate to the course work that students are undertaking and may in some cases occupy the entire session. And this is fine! However, the role of the facilitator is to lead discussions to examine topics that will expand thinking around “how we learn” and learning in general, such that, often the study circle topics are independent of a particular course. Some of the topics discussed in our groups included goal setting, learning styles, barriers to learning and time management.

5. Training and supporting the facilitators

A critical part of our project was to provide support and training for the facilitators of the Study Circles with the aim that facilitators would increasingly takes a back-seat role and the Study Circle become self-managed.

Online support

With our Centres spread across our state maintaining contact and support for them are big issues. Thus for our project we decided that EdNA Groups would provide us with a great platform to base our communications especially as it is so easy to set up!

We used the Forums for communications between our Study Circle Leaders and the Project facilitator (supported by email and teleconferences). We also used them to generate new professional discourse and to follow up from the face to face workshops that were conducted.

We used the built in Wiki facility to record the progress of the project.

We used the calendar facility to promote and keep track of project milestones and events.

And we used the file store to distribute resources such as documents and web-links.

Coordinator qualifications

In terms of coordinator or facilitator qualifications then Certificate IV in Training and Assessment is highly desirable and we need to develop a model to enable ongoing training.  So, in partnership with the Education Department’s Recognition Services which has RTO status our first group of coordinators is undertaking the TAA.

We are currently developing a Moodle website to manage and provide an online component to the course and this first group in the future will become the trainers and assessors for other coordinators undertaking the course around the state.

6. Outcomes

The outcomes for this project can be divided into those for individuals and those for the community.
For our target group, the unemployed over 45s and single parents, the outcomes are:

  • self awareness and confidence as a learner, study skills and skills for e-Learning and business.

For our Study Circle Leaders who are our Online Access Centre coordinators the outcomes are:

  • increased self awareness and skills as a lifelong learner, and
  • skills in e-Learning, training and assessment, coaching, mentoring and facilitating Study Circles.

These individual outcomes translate to outcomes for the whole community. For the eight communities involved in our project the outcomes are:

  •  new job skills in Business and IT,
  • an avenue to further education,
  • an online forum is now available for community issues, particularly in relation to adult education, and
  • there are now be more people with skills in leadership, facilitation and managing meetings.

To date we are very happy with the progress but if I was to identify one problem area I would say that it is the difficulty part-time staff, our coordinators, have in finding time for the required professional development.

There were fantastic outcomes for individuals in our project but we like to think that our greatest achievement has been the establishment of this model of support. In 2006 we have used the knowledge we gained in this project to expand the model to our entire network.

For all Communities, this project is delivering a model to build educational capacity and citizenship that will empower people to make choices and take action.
