Challenges

The learners
Providing quality, industry-recognised training to a geographically diverse workforce is a common problem for many Australian industries and businesses.  Engaging workers in training is quite another.

In a staff survey in early 2004 we found that our field operational staff have felt disadvantaged at working alongside trades qualified people, and having no opportunity to obtain a relevant National qualification themselves. So we wanted to offer them the opportunity to enroll in appropriate accredited training.

Our existing workers have a vast range of working knowledge of our industry and have embraced the opportunity to have their competencies formally recognised. 
New staff also see the career advantage of achieving a formal qualification at the outset.

The e-Learning solution
A primary motivator in SunWater’s decision to use e-learning for its training was the high cost of transporting learners from all over Queensland and interstate to a classroom setting to cover a total of around 40 different competency units relevant to the bulk water service delivery function

SunWater employees are spread from Bamaga (Far North QLD) to Scrivener Dam (ACT).  Getting everyone back for 'block training' meant time out of already busy schedules, coordination of flights, and completing the courseware using mail delivered distance material, then waiting for Trainer Assessors to become available to sign off competencies. Online training seemed to be getting more attractive by the minute. 

The course

It was important to get the right course for our target group. We completed a training needs analysis and then started to sieve through National Training Packages to determine what pathway would best fit our business and industry needs. 

Most of our staff work in remote sites, so recognition in having the skills and knowledge to make decisions about a variety of unknown problems was important.

The training focuses on core units of competency to recognise existing competency within the nationally endorsed Water Industry Training Package.  This means field workers can be formally recognised for their existing skills and can move on to further certificate level training.

Solutions

Setting up a partnership
One of the keys to starting out in a blended learning approach is to build a relationship with an RTO (registered training organisation) that understands the benefits of e-learning and has a commitment to taking e-learning to industry.  

Our field staff have, through the change management of implementing all these systems, become pretty familiar with computer technology, and almost all sites are linked to the internet or WAN. We researched training providers with industry experience and an online passion who may be willing to partner with us.  Finding the right training partner to get us up and running with e-learning was critical. Wide Bay TAFE were instrumental in getting us through this project.

 

Blended learning approach

The course is delivered online using Wide Bay TAFE’s learning management system – Janison.

We put in a lot of effort to get the online content right. All material is contextualised to SunWater’s business processes, right down to the detail of the web page matching our media style guide.  When we log onto Wide Bay's Janison site, we appear to be working in a SunWater environment.  Staff can learn when they want to, during whatever shift they work, or even after hours as the Wide Bay site is off our firewalls.

Modules in the e-learning packages include self-paced activities and formative tests to both reinforce learning and allow workers to gauge their understanding of the content.

There is plenty to keep the student involved in learning; we have quizzes, web-links, and interactive mouse activities.

The learning and assessment material is available online, and for those remote field workers without access to the web, it is also available on CD-ROM. All underpinning knowledge will be delivered by e-learning, with some classroom training provided for elective units requiring face-to-face demonstration and practice.

Customising e-learning resources
The e-learning is based on an existing Victorian CD-ROM for the water industry that had been developed with a focus on water treatment. By using much of the structure of the existing product, SunWater was able to focus on the development of content and assessment material that not only met Sunwater’s business needs but also met AQTF standards. 

The material can be easily adapted by other bulk water service providers, with the only changes required in most instances relating to organisation-specific policies, procedures and in a few areas, particular types of equipment or technology used.

Support

We knew that content by itself wouldn't do the trick. We have developed a strong mentoring structure built around regional workloads and individual learning styles.  Using Wide Bay TAFE’s Learning Management System (Janison) we are able to keep in regular touch through online forums and chat sessions using the communication tools on Janison.

We also find that it is important to encourage our learners with regular follow up and support by our training co-ordinator through email, telephone, regional site visits and newsletters. We encourage workers from different locations to chat on-line between themselves and share their learning experiences.

‘Having industry assessors and supervisors who have also taken on the role of mentor for their staff is an important part of building pride and ownership of the program within SunWater’.

Assessment

Assessment is both online and through industry assessors and supervisors, who observe competency during practise. 

‘We have staff at over 40 sites all over Queensland. By allowing field workers to complete many of their assessments online and directly relate the learning with the doing has resulted in greater engagement.

We have adopted a minimum 3 assessment approach; one is an observation checklist signed off by a supervisor with years of industry experience; another is evidence where the student completes existing workflow documents and references the correct Standards and Policies; and another uses written explanations proving their knowledge given a case scenario.  This also forms a pathway for RPL (recognition of prior learning), so using this strategy our experienced staff can ‘fast-track’ through the program.

Outcomes

Results

This is our first foray into online learning, and we offered it to our staff on a voluntary basis only.  Most of them are over 45, and all are male field workers.  We had an overwhelming response with 80% of the target group wanting to be involved.  Of the first intake of 26 students, we have had one drop out during this first phase of learning, and a waiting list has been created for future intakes.  The more experienced staff have elected not to fast track their qualification as they are enjoying the online activities and discovering that e-learning is not daunting after all.

The interest in the new training was solid evidence that the use of technology in learning need not be a barrier to a totally male workforce, which includes many mature-aged workers. 

Tips
The technology has been more of a factor in the engagement than a barrier. And making the activities in the e-learning completely relevant to their jobs, with assessments integrated into their daily work activities, further increases the engagement.

We did need to provide some additional support and coaching to the few learners who were unfamiliar with using computers to help them navigate through the material, but have been pleasantly surprised that this has not dimmed their enthusiasm.

For others in industry, I suggest talk to your Industry Skills Council and peer businesses.   Find out what worked in the past and then see if it might be different now.  Don’t be disheartened; join positive networks.  Be clear in what your end goal is and make the technology work for you.

Finding content is always an issue. Toolboxes are great starting points – there are many existing resources that someone can develop without expensive consultants and specialists.  Be open to new ideas and look at old processes with fresh, online eyes.  Adult learners are great ‘doers’ so many activities keeps their interest and they are often tricked into learning something!

‘It makes sense in particular to deliver underpinning knowledge via e-learning. You don’t have to incorporate fancy animation or sound in the e-learning material, just make sure you are producing a product based on adult learning principles and incorporate activities that will inspire the learner’,
